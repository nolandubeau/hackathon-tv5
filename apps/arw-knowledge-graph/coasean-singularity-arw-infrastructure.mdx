## The Economic Foundation of Agent-Ready Web

In 2025, economists at MIT, Harvard, and Amazon published groundbreaking research on what they call the **"Coasean Singularity"**—a tipping point where AI agents reduce transaction costs so dramatically that it fundamentally reshapes how markets operate. This isn't just theoretical economics. It's the foundation that explains why ARW (Agent-Ready Web) isn't optional infrastructure—it's essential.

## What is the Coasean Singularity?

In 1937, economist Ronald Coase asked a simple question: **Why do firms exist?** His answer: because organizing work inside a company is often cheaper than coordinating through markets. The transaction costs of searching for partners, negotiating deals, writing contracts, and enforcing agreements can exceed the benefits of specialization.

The "Coasean Singularity" represents a paradigm shift—a point where AI agents reduce these transaction costs to near-zero, making market coordination as efficient as internal organization. When transaction costs approach zero, economic structures fundamentally transform.

### Current Web: High Transaction Costs Limit Agent Capabilities

Today's web creates significant friction for AI agents:

- **Search costs**: Agents must crawl entire websites to discover capabilities
- **Communication costs**: Parse 55KB of HTML to extract 8KB of actual content
- **Contracting costs**: No standard protocols for agent-to-website transactions
- **Verification costs**: No way to track or verify agent behavior
- **Identity costs**: Can't distinguish legitimate agents from malicious bots

**Result:** High transaction costs severely limit what agents can accomplish on your behalf.

### With ARW: Transaction Costs Approach Near-Zero

ARW systematically eliminates these friction points:

- **Search costs**: ↓ 90% via structured discovery (`llms.txt` manifest)
- **Communication costs**: ↓ 85% via machine views (`.llm.md` files)
- **Contracting costs**: ↓ 80% via declarative actions with OAuth
- **Verification costs**: ↓ 70% via observability headers (`AI-*` headers)
- **Identity costs**: ↓ 95% via standardized agent identification

**Result:** Transaction costs approach the "singularity" threshold where agent-mediated commerce becomes economically viable at scale.

## What This Means for the Web Economy

The transition to near-zero transaction costs isn't gradual—it's a phase change. Just as HTTPS became non-negotiable for e-commerce, ARW will become non-negotiable for agent commerce.

### New Market Designs Previously Infeasible

**1. Micro-Niche Marketplaces**
Lower search costs mean agents can efficiently match long-tail preferences. A market for "left-handed acoustic guitars under $800 with specific wood types" becomes economically viable.

**2. Micropayment Economies**
Lower contracting costs make sub-dollar transactions practical. Pay $0.03 to read a single article, $0.15 for a single API call.

**3. Tiered Access Markets**
Fine-grained identity verification enables differential pricing: inference queries might be free, training data might cost more, premium agents might pay for priority access.

### The Open Infrastructure Advantage

Some economists worry that transaction costs won't vanish—they'll just mutate into "access rents" where platforms charge for premium connections.

**ARW prevents this rent-seeking through open standards:**

- MIT-licensed specification (no proprietary gatekeepers)
- Universal protocol (any agent can access any ARW site)
- No approval required (permissionless innovation)

This is why ARW being open source isn't idealistic—it's economically critical. Open infrastructure prevents the Coasean Singularity from turning into a Coasean Monopoly.
